Adolescent health in India — the big picture
India has 253 million adolescents — the largest in the world. Their health is not just a medical challenge; it is a systems challenge shaped by education, gender, economics, culture, and governance all at once.

What makes adolescent health a systems problem
Why systems thinking — and not just programme logic?
Most public health programmes are designed with linear logic: an input causes an output. But adolescent health doesn't work that way. Understanding the difference is the first step to systems change.

Programme thinking (linear)

  • One problem → one solution
  • Cause leads directly to effect
  • Inputs → outputs → outcomes
  • Expert designs, community implements
  • Plan, then execute, then evaluate
  • Success = reaching targets
  • Unintended consequences = failure
  • Scale up the intervention
VS

Systems thinking (non-linear)

  • Many causes → many effects, often delayed
  • Circular causality, feedback loops
  • Structure drives behaviour
  • Stakeholders co-design solutions
  • Plan → act → sense → adapt
  • Success = system capacity to evolve
  • Unintended consequences = data to learn from
  • Scale the CONDITIONS, not the programme
